ANALYTICAL REPORT ON THE VEGETABLE OILS AND FATS MARKET
KEY EVENTS 7 – 14 JUNE, 2026
Regional Coverage: European Union (EU-27), United Kingdom, Balkan Region, Central Asia, and the Caucasus.

I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY 
In June 2026, the vegetable oil market is showing a pronounced correction due to an oversupply from South America and the active export policies of Asian countries. For the Central Asia and Caucasus region, the key trend is a shift from simple processing to deep integration into global supply chains, which requires strict compliance with traceability standards (EUDR). Despite the decline in global commodity prices, logistics premiums in the Black Sea region remain high, creating a price gap between raw materials and finished products.
The key trends of the reporting period are structured as follows:
•	Global Price Normalization: A shift towards a bearish trend in palm and soybean oils due to record harvests in South America and policy adjustments in Indonesia.
•	Logistics Risk Premium: Persistent divergence between high physical delivery costs in the Black Sea region and the optimism surrounding record harvest forecasts (Russia/Ukraine).
•	Regional Industrial Transformation: Central Asia and the Caucasus are pivoting from import dependency toward localized value-added processing (refined oil, meal) to ensure food security and export revenues.
•	Compliance as a Market Barrier: The implementation of EUDR and RED III mandates is creating a "regulatory filter," where traceability and ISCC certification become essential prerequisites for accessing premium European markets.
•	Strategic Logistics Diversification: Increased reliance on regional corridors, particularly the Trans-Caspian route, as a critical infrastructure response to geopolitical instability in traditional trade channels.

III. REGIONAL MARKETS ANALYSIS 
1. Major Western and Northern European Markets
The market situation in Western and Northern Europe as of mid-June 2026 is characterized by growth in oilseed production while maintaining high volatility in energy prices. 
Germany
Germany remains a key consumer of vegetable oils for the HVO (Hydrotreated Vegetable Oil) sector. Amidst transport decarbonization and the implementation of the RED III strategy, the German market shows sustained interest in UCO (Used Cooking Oil) and technical fats. According to UFOP data, oilseed acreage in Germany is stable, although farmers maintain cautious optimism regarding yields due to weather factors. In the food segment, there is a distinct trend towards the consumption of cold-pressed and organic oils. 
Netherlands
The Netherlands acts as Europe's primary logistics and processing hub. In June 2026, the country's main focus is on preparing for EUDR (EU Deforestation Regulation) requirements. Importers are actively adapting contracts to ensure feedstock traceability. There is growing demand for non-GMO labeled oils, which dictates new requirements for suppliers from Latin America and Southeast Asia. 
United Kingdom
According to current forecasts (USDA/FAS), rapeseed acreage in the UK is recovering by 14-15% compared to last year in the 2026/27 marketing year. Production is expected to exceed 1 million tonnes for the first time since 2023. The RTFO (Renewable Transport Fuel Obligation) policy limits the use of food-grade oils in biofuel (up to 3%), redirecting additional rapeseed oil volumes to the food and feed industries.
France
France, one of the EU's leading rapeseed producers, reports favorable conditions for crop development in June. The industry is focused on increasing processing efficiency and integrating innovations in the second-generation biodiesel production sector. There is a tendency towards consolidating local supply chains to minimize logistics risks. 
Belgium
Belgium retains significant oil refining capacities. The Antwerp cluster is actively involved in the transshipment of vegetable oils imported for the needs of all Northwestern Europe. In June 2026, the focus is on managing feedstock quality and adhering to environmental standards during transit operations. In the industrial sector, there is an integration of chemical and food supply chains within the framework of pan-European environmental initiatives. 
Ireland
The Irish vegetable oil market is tightly integrated into the pan-European supply network. Primary demand in the country is driven by the food industry and the HoReCa sector. As of June 2026, stable imports of rapeseed and sunflower oil from continental Europe are noted, with retailers strengthening controls over product composition in response to pan-European trends regarding transparency in fat labeling. 
Market Overview (Western Europe)
According to the European Commission (DG AGRI), oilseed production in the EU-27 for the 2026/27 season is projected at 32.3 million tonnes (+3.3% year-on-year), driven by expanded sunflower and rapeseed acreage. Domestic processing is increasing, leading to reduced dependence on "crude" oil imports (-25% compared to the 5-year average). A key price factor for the region remains the "sustainability premium" within the framework of EUDR compliance.
2. Southern, Central, and Eastern European Regional Markets 
Italy
The Italian energy sector is undergoing a transition to advanced waste-based feedstocks in line with RED III mandates. Domestic biorefineries are reducing their reliance on traditional food-grade palm oil in favor of used cooking oil (UCO) and agricultural residues to meet emission reduction targets. 
Poland
Following a period of drought in May, rainfall in early June has significantly improved the outlook for winter rapeseed. The Polish agricultural sector is currently stabilizing, with industry associations projecting a recovery in yields, although market participants remain cautious regarding the impact of input price fluctuations on final production costs. 
Czech Republic
Regulatory activity in the Czech Republic is focused on consumer transparency. Recent amendments to food decrees have established stricter requirements for the classification and labeling of spreadable fats and mixed products. Retail chains are increasingly segregating vegetable-based spreads from animal-based butter to comply with updated labeling standards and improve shelf-level transparency. 
Estonia
The Estonian market is characterized by high integration into the Baltic energy and food systems. Due to the limited size of domestic processing facilities, the market remains reliant on regional imports. In June 2026, the primary focus is on ensuring a stable supply of edible oils through harmonized regional logistics, aimed at mitigating price spikes for the consumer market. 
Greece
The Greek market is actively exploring the potential of biofuel-based bunkering for the maritime sector. Despite water stress in the southern regions of the country, sunflower cultivation remains a strategic priority for domestic producers aiming to reduce dependence on external imports, while government policy continues to support regional self-sufficiency initiatives.
4. Production and Balances in the Black Sea Region
The situation in the Black Sea region in June 2026 is characterized by a recovery in production volumes after two years of deficit; however, logistics and regulatory factors continue to exert a significant influence on trade flows. 
Ukraine
Production: According to USDA forecasts for the 2026/27 season, Ukraine is showing steady growth in sunflower production, up to 13.5 million tonnes, which is 23% higher than the previous season's figures (11 million tonnes). The sown area for sunflowers has expanded to 5.4 million hectares (+6%). 
Processing and Export: Significant recovery in processing capacities is projected: sunflower oil output may reach 5.63 million tonnes (+19% compared to the 2025/26 MY). The export potential for oil is estimated at 5.1 million tonnes (+16.6% year-on-year). Despite external challenges, Ukraine maintains its status as a leading global exporter of sunflower oil, actively using maritime corridors for shipments to EU countries and Asia.
Russia
Production: Russia retains the lead in gross oilseed harvest. The forecast for sunflower production for the 2026/27 season is 19.2 million tonnes (+10% compared to last year). Sunflower oil output is expected to be at the level of 7.52 million tonnes.
Regulation and Export: Export policy remains a tool for containing domestic prices. As of June 2026, the export duty on sunflower oil was significantly reduced (to 1,337.5 Rubles per tonne), which is aimed at supporting producers' export revenues. Nevertheless, the "oil damper" mechanism and high export duties continue to limit volumes of crude oil exports, stimulating processors to increase margins domestically. 
Balance: Sunflower oil exports from the RF are projected at 4.85 million tonnes, which is slightly behind Ukrainian figures, despite a higher volume of seed production. The RF market shows a trend towards diversifying sales destinations, reorienting towards India and China. 
Regional Overview and Logistics
Market Environment: Sunflower oil prices in the region remain at elevated levels due to the structural deficit of stocks accumulated over previous seasons. In June 2026, a "bearish" sentiment is observed for future periods, driven by expectations of a high harvest; however, the current spot situation remains tight. 
Logistics Risks: Freight and insurance costs remain key variables. Despite favorable weather conditions at the start of summer 2026, any deviations from normal port operations in the Black Sea are immediately reflected in market premiums, which makes Black Sea FOB quotes volatile.
4. Central Asia and the Caucasus
The Central Asia and Caucasus region is demonstrating an active transformation phase in the oil and fat industry in June 2026, driven by countries' aspirations for import substitution and the growth of export potential for processed products. 
Kazakhstan
Market Position: Kazakhstan is a key oilseed producer in the region. As of June 2026, the country shows record export revenues from oil and fat products (approximately $963 million over 4 years).
Logistics: In 2026, the first shipments of vegetable oil directly onto marine vessels were implemented at the port of Aktau, opening new routes across the Caspian Sea. Kazakhstan is actively developing processing, shifting from exporting raw materials to exporting finished oil and meal, with a focus on markets in China and Central Asian countries. 
Uzbekistan
Production: There is steady growth in processing volumes. In the first quarter of 2026, major enterprises increased the output of refined vegetable oil by more than 11% compared to the same period last year. 
Technology: The industry is actively adopting modern energy-saving technologies, including heat recovery systems and extraction plant optimization, which allows for effective adaptation to the region's climatic conditions. 
Kyrgyzstan
Self-sufficiency: The government continues to implement its import substitution policy. As of June 2026, the self-sufficiency level for vegetable oil exceeded 56%, a result of expanding processing capacities and introducing new high-yielding oilseed varieties (including safflower). 
Tajikistan
Market Balance: The country remains dependent on imports of vegetable oils and raw materials for meal production. Main trade flows are directed toward imports from neighboring Kyrgyzstan and regional EAEU partners, while integration into regional logistics chains is observed to ensure supply stability. 
Turkmenistan 
Features: The market is characterized by a high degree of state regulation. The primary focus is on developing the domestic agro-industrial complex to meet the needs of the internal market. Information on foreign trade volumes is limited; the main trend is the development of local production. 
Azerbaijan
Sector Development: Azerbaijan demonstrates a confident recovery in non-oil economic sectors. In the first half of 2026, significant growth in agricultural exports was recorded. The agro-industrial complex is actively utilizing existing processing capacities, allowing the country to reduce its dependence on imports of finished food fats and oils. 
Georgia
Infrastructure: Georgia plays the role of an important transit hub. In 2026, volumes of transshipment and export of oil products through port infrastructure grew significantly, which indirectly facilitates the development of the general logistics network, also used for the transport of food cargo. Port capacities, such as Kulevi, are actively integrating into international trade chains.
Regional Overview and Logistics
Trends: Countries in the region are actively transitioning from a pure import model to the development of their own processing capabilities. In 2026, ensuring stable logistics and access to external markets remains a key challenge.
Risks: Fluctuations in exchange rates and the geopolitical situation continue to exert pressure on the cost of imported components and finished products, stimulating the development of regional trade ties within Central Asia and the Caucasus.

III REGULATORY OUTLOOK
In mid-2026, the regulatory environment for Central Asian and Caucasian exporters to the EU is governed by the implementation of EUDR (Deforestation Regulation) and RED III (Renewable Energy Directive). 
EUDR Compliance: Following the May 2026 simplification package, large and medium-sized operators must be fully compliant by December 30, 2026, while micro and small enterprises have until June 30, 2027. The regulation mandates rigorous traceability, requiring geolocation data for every plot of land producing relevant commodities (soy, palm oil derivatives, etc.). Exporters in the region must digitize their supply chains to provide auditable proof of "deforestation-free" status.
RED III & Sustainability: The directive continues to tighten sustainability criteria for biofuels. New regulations regarding MOAH/MOSH limits in food oils and fats (effective 2026–2027) necessitate stricter monitoring of processing aids and packaging materials.
Regional Impact: While these regulations primarily target EU importers, they act as a "regulatory filter" for producers in Central Asia and the Caucasus. Those intending to export to European markets must invest in robust internal traceability systems to avoid exclusion, as non-compliance risks shipment blockages and significant fines. Local producers are increasingly aligning with international standards (e.g., ISCC) to maintain market access.

V. CONCLUSION: IMPLICATIONS FOR THE MALAYSIAN PALM OIL INDUSTRY
Re-export to Ukraine and Georgia from Turkey: It is necessary to develop alternative routes for the re-export of Malaysian palm oil from Turkey towards Ukraine (the import potential exceeds 200,000 tons per year), Georgia, and Azerbaijan.
Creating a Strategic Hub in Tashkent: Expedite the consideration of the official proposal from Uzbekistan’s Ambassador Karomidin Gadoev to establish a regional distribution logistics warehouse for Malaysian oils in Tashkent. The warehouse should be operational and stocked before the scheduled review of customs duties in the CIS, planned for 2027.
"Counter-Labeling" Campaign in Serbia: Turn the mandatory implementation of the "yellow triangle" in the Balkans into a marketing advantage. Launch an targeted promotional campaign among Serbian B2B clients, positioning certified palm oil (MSPO) not as a cheap substitute, but as an eco-friendly, stable, heart-healthy, and drought-protected alternative to European soft oils facing severe climate pressure.
Filling the Uzbek Industrial Vacuum: Leverage the fact that 63% of processing capacities in Uzbekistan are currently idle. Offer direct contracts for the supply of Malaysian palm oil as a primary feedstock for local margarine production, effectively bridging their gap.
Shifting Premium Fats to the Middle Corridor (TITR): Diversify supply chains for specialty fats bound for the confectionery industry. Redirect them from the ocean route (where spot freight has soared to $10,000) to the land-based Middle Corridor with its guaranteed 15-day transit and stable insurance environment.
