III. REGIONAL MARKETS ANALYSIS

1. Major Western and Northern European Markets

Preparations continue for the entry into force of key requirements (December 30, 2026). Regulatory authorities have issued updated guidelines for operators, confirming that all products containing palm and soy oil (as well as derived confectionery fats) are subject to strict verification of their deforestation-free status. Simplified procedures have been introduced for small enterprises, but the core requirements for geolocation and traceability remain unchanged.

In June, the FAO Food Price Index averaged 130.3 points, down 0.3% from May, as price increases for vegetable oils and meat were offset by declines in sugar, cereals, and dairy products. Although the index is 1.7% higher than in June 2025, it remains 18.7% below the March 2022 peak. The cereal price index fell by 3.5% to 110.2 points, driven by a 4.4% drop in wheat prices—due to active harvesting and favorable supply prospects in the Black Sea region despite concerns in the US and Australia—and a 6.2% decrease in corn prices resulting from ample South American supplies and lower crude oil prices. This downward trend was further influenced by a stronger US dollar and easing energy costs linked to reduced tensions in the Strait of Hormuz. Meanwhile, the vegetable oil price index rose 3.8% to 192 points, a 23.3% increase year-on-year, as rising palm and rapeseed oil quotations more than compensated for lower soybean oil prices.

The weather in Western Europe remains the main factor influencing agricultural prices, as hot and dry conditions continue to support grain and oilseed markets and keep traders focused on crop prospects in key producing areas. At the same time, market participants are watching weather developments closely because any further deterioration could add support to rapeseed, wheat, and other agricultural quotations, while improved conditions would likely ease some of the upward pressure.

During the week ending July 2, 2026, European biofuel feedstock prices exhibited mixed trends, with Used Cooking Oil (UCO) rising to €1,180 per tonne due to high vegetable oil costs and reduced Asian imports. While animal fat prices remained varied amid supply constraints for lower categories and a potential decline in US export demand, advanced feedstocks like POME and food waste oil faced sluggish trading and poor production margins. Despite some higher price indications, market participants characterized current levels for advanced materials as "unreasonably high" given the low demand for final biofuel products.

UK

The U.K. government has announced the introduction of delayed regulations requiring companies to check the supply chains of commodities such as soy, palm oil, cocoa, and rubber for links to illegal deforestation. These measures, based on the Environment Act of 2021, aim to align U.K. legislation with the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) to facilitate trade and address issues with Northern Ireland. However, the U.K. rules currently only address deforestation deemed illegal in the exporting country, whereas EU regulations prohibit any form of deforestation. Updated regulations are expected to be submitted to Parliament in 2027.

The UK government has decided to maintain anti-dumping duties on Indonesian biodiesel imports for another five years, upholding tariffs ranging from 8% to 18% following a recommendation by the Trade Remedies Authority. This decision concludes one of the final transition reviews of trade protections inherited from the EU post-Brexit, ensuring continued support for domestic producers. While the duties remain in place for road transport fuels, the updated regulations now specifically exclude sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) from the product scope, reflecting differences in production processes and market requirements.

2. Southern, Central, and Eastern European Regional Markets

Romania

Argentina has exported a record 1.3 million metric tons of sunflower seeds this season, with nearly 47% of that volume—over 605,000 metric tons—destined for Bulgaria and Romania, and an additional 98,000 metric tons for Turkey. This surge in large-scale shipments from South America, transported via Handysize and Supramax vessels, has significantly altered the competitive landscape in the Black Sea region, which is traditionally dominated by local producers from countries like Moldova and Ukraine. Consequently, as Bulgarian and Romanian processors increasingly opt for these bulk imports at competitive prices, the demand and price premiums for regional sunflower seeds, particularly from the Republic of Moldova, are facing significant downward pressure.

Moldova

In the first five months of 2026, Moldova emerged as the leading importer of Ukrainian agricultural products, accounting for 42.4% of its butter exports and purchasing 14,900 tons of wheat flour. Despite Moldova's dominant market share, Ukraine's overall butter exports faced a significant downturn, dropping 2.1 times in volume to 3,800 tons compared to the same period in 2025 due to low global demand. While other key buyers include Azerbaijan and Georgia for dairy and Palestine and Czechia for flour, Ukraine continues to diversify its trade, recently opening the Moldovan market to live snails and snail roe.

4. Production and Balances in the Black Sea Region

Ukraine

Sunflower seed prices in Ukraine remain at a high level, with purchase prices holding at UAH 33,000–33,500/t delivered to plants, but they may start to ease in July as the number of sunflower-processing plants declines with the start of the rapeseed season. Tight supply at the end of the season has also pushed sunflower oil prices to their highest level of the 2025/26 season, at $1,335–1,340/t delivered to port, while dry weather in the west and heat across the country could reduce yield potential in low-moisture regions; however, forecast rains next week may improve crop conditions and add pressure to new-crop prices.

Rapeseed prices in Ukraine are falling under pressure from weaker exchange quotations, while farmers are holding back sales, with export prices down $10/t over the week to $540–560/t depending on oil content and processor bids at UAH 25,500–26,500/t delivered to plants. A heatwave in Europe accelerated rapeseed maturation but sharply reduced yield potential, especially in France and Germany, and may also lower yields in Poland and Ukraine, even as lower oil prices continue to weigh on the market; at the same time, demand from processors is still supporting rapeseed prices in July because crushers need raw material before sunflower processing ramps up.

The free trade agreement between Ukraine and the UAE has entered into force. As of 1 July, the Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement between the Government of Ukraine and the Government of the United Arab Emirates, signed on 17 February 2025 and ratified by Ukraine’s Law No. 4802-IX on 26 February 2026, has taken effect, according to Ukraine’s State Customs Service. The agreement provides a modern framework for the development of bilateral trade in goods and services and creates a free trade area between Ukraine and the UAE through the removal of tariffs, giving Ukrainian producers preferential access to the UAE market and helping expand the export geography of domestic products. It also deepens cooperation between the customs administrations of Ukraine and the UAE, including plans to conclude a mutual customs assistance agreement that would form the basis for information exchange and for preventing and investigating violations of customs legislation.

Russia

In July, the export duty on sunflower oil in the Russian Federation will increase to 3,294.2 rubles per ton compared to the 1,337.5 rubles per ton in effect this month, the Russian Ministry of Agriculture reported. The customs duty on the export of Russian sunflower meal next month, as in June, will be 0 rubles per ton. The ministry also added that export duties for the next month were calculated based on an indicative price of $1,297.2 per ton (in June - $1,292.3 per ton) for sunflower oil and $215 ($196.1) per ton for sunflower meal.

4. Central Asia and the Caucasus

Economic cooperation between Central Asia and the Gulf states is expanding rapidly, with cumulative investments reaching $23 billion, according to the Eurasian Development Bank (EDB). Evgeny Vinokurov, EDB’s Chief Economist, noted that while the UAE remains the largest investor, Saudi Arabia and Qatar are steadily increasing their activity, and Oman is focusing on logistics infrastructure such as dry ports and warehouses. Central Asia maintains its investment appeal, having averaged 6% annual economic growth over the last 25 years—a trend projected to persist in the medium term. In line with these developments, the EDB has strengthened its regional presence by opening an office in the Abu Dhabi Global Market (ADGM) and beginning to raise financing in UAE dirhams.

Kazakhstan

Kazakhstan is forecast to have a good sunflower harvest in 2026, according to Kazhydromet, which said soil moisture reserves were generally adequate and the agrometeorological conditions support crop development. Based on preliminary calculations, sunflower yields are expected to be above the multi-year average in parts of Kostanay, Pavlodar, Abai, and East Kazakhstan regions, while in other districts they should be around normal levels.

Talgat Aldybergenov, Chairman of the Management Board of NC KTZ JSC, held a meeting with Merle Kurvits, Chairman of the Management Board of Estonian Railways, to discuss expanding freight traffic, modernizing infrastructure, and establishing new logistics services between Central Asia and Europe. During the talks, the parties reviewed cooperation with Estonian seaports and outlined steps to increase cargo volumes along the Kazakhstan–Baltics–Europe route, reaffirming their mutual interest in long-term cooperation and joint projects within the Trans-Caspian International Transport Route (TITR). Special attention was also paid to digitalization, infrastructure monitoring, the development of multimodal transportation, and the attraction of new freight flows.

Uzbekistan

The population of Uzbekistan has exceeded 39 million people, according to preliminary census results announced by the head of the National Statistics Committee, Behzod Hamraev, on June 30, 2026. As of January 15, 2026, the actual population was reported to be 39,047,321, which is 810,617 more than current statistical estimates of 38.24 million. Of this population, 19.77 million (50.6%) are men and 19.28 million (49.4%) are women, with Uzbeks making up 89.4% (34.9 million) of the population, followed by Tajiks (1.28 million), Karakalpaks (841,400), Kazakhs (707,330), and Russians (606,530). The Uzbek language is reported as the native language of 35.7 million people (91.3% of the population). The urban population stands at 21.3 million (54.5%), while 17.8 million (45.5%) live in rural areas. The census covered 97.3% of the population, with the remaining 2.7% not participating for various reasons, which is considered acceptable by international standards. Preliminary data indicates that Uzbekistan's population has doubled since the 1989 census.

Georgia

Presidents Kassym-Jomart Tokayev of Kazakhstan and Irakli Kobakhidze of Georgia have signed a joint statement establishing a strategic partnership, with Tokayev proposing a comprehensive roadmap focusing on energy, investment, transport, agriculture, digitalization, and tourism. Both nations reaffirmed their commitment to mutual investment projects and the development of the Trans-Caspian International Transport Route (TITR) through infrastructure upgrades, expanded container shipping, and harmonized tariff policies. Given that transit volumes on the TITR have increased 3.5 times over the last five years and are projected to reach 3,000 container trains by 2029, Kazakhstan is looking to expand its maritime presence in Georgia beyond the port of Batumi, specifically targeting opportunities in the Poti multimodal terminal and the port of Anaklia to further strengthen regional connectivity.

IV. LOGISTICS AND SUPPLY SECURITY: COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

Uzbekistan aims to become a "real transit hub," as stated by Transport Minister Ilhom Mahkamov, who reported a 43% increase in the total cargo turnover and a 35% rise in transit during the first half of the year. With the construction of new transport corridors, the country needs to expand its roads, railway sections, and logistics centers to accelerate its transformation into a genuine transit hub. President Shavkat Mirziyoyev reviewed proposals for developing Uzbekistan's transport and logistics system on July 1, emphasizing the importance of enhancing the country's transit potential. Mahkamov highlighted the necessity of creating transport and logistics infrastructure to support trade volumes, ensure the continuity and security of cargo turnover, and facilitate international freight transport. He noted that the development of logistics centers is directly linked to these efforts, with 27 centers currently operating, although not all meet the required standards. The government is working on additional measures to support the establishment of new class "A" international logistics centers, aiming to attract more entrepreneurs to the sector.

Experts at the Tashkent International Investment Forum highlighted that while the Middle Corridor (Trans-Caspian International Transport Route) has seen a nearly fivefold increase in volume over the past five years, its future success depends on transitioning from a fragmented collection of national projects into a unified, predictable, and digitally integrated system. Beyond physical infrastructure like the China-Kyrgyzstan-Uzbekistan railway, the corridor faces critical "soft" bottlenecks, including inconsistent customs procedures, a lack of synchronized digital documentation, and a shortage of high-quality logistics hubs. To effectively compete with the simpler Northern Route and reach its full potential of 1.4 million TEU, participating countries—with Uzbekistan positioned as a central hub—must prioritize coordinated investments and harmonized standards to ensure the route operates as a seamless "Point A to Point B" service for global business.

The European Union has officially committed to large-scale financing for the Trans-Caspian Transport Corridor, mobilizing up to €2 billion to strengthen strategic links between Europe and Central Asia through the newly launched Connectivity Agenda Platform. During a high-level meeting in Brussels involving EU commissioners, G7 representatives, and ministers from Central Asia, the Caucasus, and Eastern Europe, participants agreed to coordinate investments in transport, energy, and digital sectors to bypass traditional northern routes via a corridor through the Black Sea and South Caucasus. This investment aims to modernize critical infrastructure, upgrade border crossings, and simplify trade procedures, with the goal of increasing trade volumes five-fold over the next 15 years and positioning Kazakhstan as a central hub. European officials framed the project as an urgent response to geopolitical pressures and shifting logistics.

V. CONCLUSION: IMPLICATIONS FOR THE MALAYSIAN PALM OIL INDUSTRY

Re-export to Ukraine and Georgia from Turkey: It is necessary to develop alternative routes for the re-export of Malaysian palm oil from Turkey towards Ukraine, Georgia, and Azerbaijan.

Creating a Strategic Hub in Tashkent: Expedite the consideration of the official proposal from Uzbekistan’s Ambassador Karomidin Gadoev to establish a regional distribution logistics warehouse for Malaysian oils in Tashkent.

Shifting Premium Fats to the Middle Corridor (TITR): Diversify supply chains for specialty fats bound for the confectionery industry. Redirect them from the ocean route (where spot freight has soared to $10,000) to the land-based Middle Corridor with its guaranteed 15-day transit and stable insurance environment.
